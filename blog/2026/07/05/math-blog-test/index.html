This temporary note checks the typography for mathematical exposition: paragraphs, inline formulae, displayed equations, statements, proofs, remarks, references, and code.

A Small Example

For an inline example, let \(f \in L^2(\mathbb{T})\). Its Fourier coefficients are
\(\widehat f(n)=\int_{\mathbb{T}} f(x)e^{-2\pi i n x}\,dx\).

Displayed formulae should have enough room to breathe:

\[\|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{T})}^2 = \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} |\widehat f(n)|^2.\]

Definition. A dyadic decomposition is a family of smooth Fourier multipliers \(\{P_k\}_{k\geq 0}\) localizing a function to successive frequency scales.

Lemma. Let \(\{P_k\}_{k\geq 0}\) be a smooth dyadic decomposition of frequency space. For \(1<p<\infty\), one expects a square-function estimate of the form

\[\left\|\left(\sum_{k\geq 0} |P_k f|^2\right)^{1/2}\right\|_{L^p} \lesssim_p \|f\|_{L^p}.\]

Proof. This is only a layout test, but a proof paragraph should feel quieter than the statement itself. It may contain inline formulae such as \(P_k f\) and should return naturally to the surrounding text. □

Remark. Remarks should be visually lighter than lemmas or theorems. They are useful for comments, examples, and warnings that should not compete with the main statements.

After a statement or proof, the text should return to the normal reading rhythm. Long formulae should scroll on small screens rather than forcing the page wider:

\[\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}\int_{\mathbb{R}^d} \frac{|u(x)-u(y)|^2}{|x-y|^{d+2s}}\,dx\,dy \simeq \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} |\xi|^{2s}|\widehat u(\xi)|^2\,d\xi.\]
